1. Net Force (\(F_{net}\))

The net force acting on an object is the vector sum of all individual forces applied. It determines the acceleration and overall motion of the object.

\[ F_{net} = \sum F_i \] where \(F_{net}\) = net force vector, and \(\sum F_i\) = sum of all applied forces.

Intuition: If multiple people push a box in different directions, the box will move in the direction of the combined net force.

2. Newton‚Äôs Three Laws of Motion

First Law ‚Äì Law of Inertia

An object will remain at rest or move with constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force. This law formalizes the concept of inertia.

Second Law ‚Äì Law of Acceleration

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force applied and inversely proportional to its mass:

\[ F_{net} = m \cdot a \] where \(m\) = mass (kg), \(a\) = acceleration (\(m/s^2\)).

Example: A 10 kg object under a 20 N net force accelerates at \(a = F/m = 2 \, m/s^2\).

Third Law ‚Äì Action and Reaction

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always come in pairs and act on different objects.

\[ F_{AB} = -F_{BA} \] where \(F_{AB}\) is the force exerted on A by B, and \(F_{BA}\) is the force exerted on B by A.

Example: When you push against a wall, the wall pushes back with equal magnitude.

3. Types of Forces

  • Normal Force (N): Perpendicular force exerted by a surface on an object in contact. On a flat surface, it often balances the weight.
  • Frictional Force (f): Opposes motion between two surfaces.
    • Static friction: \[ f_s \leq \mu_s N \] Prevents motion up to a maximum threshold.
    • Kinetic friction: \[ f_k = \mu_k N \] Acts on moving objects, usually smaller than static friction.
  • Tension (T): Pulling force transmitted along a rope or cable, equal at both ends if the rope is massless and frictionless.
  • Spring Force (\(F_s\)): Governed by Hooke‚Äôs Law: \[ F_s = -k x \] where \(k\) = spring constant, \(x\) = displacement from equilibrium.
  • Weight (W): The gravitational force acting on a mass: \[ W = m g \] where \(g \approx 9.8 m/s^2\).

4. Free-Body Diagram (FBD)

A free-body diagram isolates an object and represents all forces acting upon it with arrows. Each arrow‚Äôs length indicates magnitude, and its direction shows the force‚Äôs orientation. Acceleration is not drawn as a force, but it is inferred from the net force.

üí° Example: A block on a table may have arrows for weight (down), normal force (up), and applied force (horizontal).

5. Connection to Kinematics

Dynamics (force and Newton‚Äôs laws) explains why motion occurs, while kinematics describes how motion evolves. By applying Newton‚Äôs second law, we find acceleration; this acceleration is then used in kinematic equations to determine velocity, position, and displacement over time.

Example workflow: Force analysis ‚Üí Net force ‚Üí Acceleration ‚Üí Use kinematics (\(v = v_0 + at\)) to solve for motion.
