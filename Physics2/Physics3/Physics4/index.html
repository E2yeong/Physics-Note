1) Work

Posted on 2025.09.18

Definition: When a force acts on an object and displaces it by distance \(d\) in the direction of the force, the work done is:

\[ W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos \theta \]

  • \(F\): magnitude of the applied force
  • \(d\): displacement of the object
  • \(\theta\): angle between force and displacement

Characteristics

  • Positive Work: force and displacement in same direction → energy increases.
  • Negative Work: force opposes displacement → energy decreases (e.g., friction).
  • Zero Work: force ⟂ displacement (\(\cos 90^\circ = 0\)). Example: centripetal force in uniform circular motion.

Examples

  • Pushing a box horizontally: \(W = Fd\).
  • Uniform circular motion: centripetal force does zero work.

2) Types of Energy

2.1 Kinetic Energy (KE)

\[ KE = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \]

Energy of motion. Doubling velocity → quadruples kinetic energy.

2.2 Potential Energy (PE)

  • Gravitational: \( PE_g = mgh \)
  • Elastic: \( PE_s = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 \)
    where \(k\) = spring constant, \(x\) = extension/compression.

Stored energy associated with conservative forces (gravity, springs).

2.3 Mechanical Energy

\[ E_{mech} = KE + PE \]

Total of kinetic and potential energies. Conserved when only conservative forces act.

3) Energy Conservation & Work–Energy Theorem

3.1 Conservation of Mechanical Energy

If no non-conservative forces act:

\[ E_{mech,initial} = E_{mech,final} \]

Example: In free fall, loss of \(PE_g\) = gain in \(KE\).

3.2 Work–Energy Theorem

The net work done by all forces equals the change in kinetic energy:

\[ W_{net} = \Delta KE \]

With non-conservative forces:

\[ \Delta E_{mech} = W_{nc} \]

Conservative forces only change the form of energy (KE ↔ PE). Non-conservative forces (friction, drag) reduce total mechanical energy.

4) Power

Definition: The rate of doing work or transferring energy.

  • Average Power: \[ P = \frac{W}{t} \]
  • Instantaneous Power: \[ P = F \cdot v \cdot \cos \theta \]

Unit: Watt (W), where \(1 \text{ W} = 1 \text{ J/s}\).

Examples

  • Elevator lifting mass \(m\) at speed \(v\): \(P = mgv\).
  • A 60 W bulb converts 60 J of energy every second into light and heat.

5) Applications & Key Exam Concepts

  • Sign of Work: Determined by angle between force and displacement.
  • Conservative vs Non-conservative:
    • Conservative (gravity, springs): path independent, energy conserved.
    • Non-conservative (friction, resistance): path dependent, energy dissipated.
  • Velocity–Position–Energy link: Conservation laws allow solving for velocity without kinematics (e.g., pendulum, inclined plane).
  • Power problems: Cars, elevators, power consumption are frequent applications.
Exam Checklist: Identify forces, check if conservative, write energy equations, apply work–energy theorem, and calculate power where needed.
