Simple Harmonic Motion — Mass on a Spring

Understanding oscillatory motion through Hooke’s Law and Newton’s Second Law

Posted on 2025.11.06

Concept

A mass attached to a spring moves back and forth around an equilibrium position due to a restoring force proportional to its displacement.

Hooke’s Law

F = -k x
  • F: restoring force (N)
  • k: spring constant (N/m)
  • x: displacement from equilibrium (m)

The negative sign (–) means the force always acts toward the equilibrium point.

Equation of Motion (Newton’s Second Law)

F = ma
ma = -k x
a = - (k/m) x

Since acceleration is proportional to –x, this motion is classified as Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM).

General Solution

x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)
  • A: amplitude
  • ω: angular frequency = √(k/m)
  • φ: phase constant (depends on initial conditions)

Velocity and Acceleration

v(t) = -Aω sin(ωt + φ)
a(t) = -Aω² cos(ωt + φ)

Alternatively, since x = A cos(ωt + φ), acceleration can also be written as:

a = -ω² x

Period and Frequency

T = 2π √(m / k)
f = 1 / T = (1 / 2π) √(k / m)

T is the time for one complete oscillation, and f is the number of oscillations per second.

Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

Total Energy (E) = ½ k A²

Energy Components

  • Potential Energy: U = ½ k x²
  • Kinetic Energy: K = ½ k (A² - x²)
PositionKinetic Energy (K)Potential Energy (U)
At equilibrium (x = 0)Maximum0
At extreme (x = ±A)0Maximum

Key idea: Total mechanical energy remains constant; it simply oscillates between potential and kinetic forms.
