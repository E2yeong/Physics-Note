Momentum, Impulse, and Collisions

A Comprehensive Physics Study Guide

Posted on 2025.09.25

1. Momentum

Momentum (p) is a vector quantity defined as the product of mass and velocity:

p = m · v
  • Vector nature: Direction matters. Two objects with equal speed but opposite directions have opposite momenta.
  • Units: kg·m/s (SI units).
  • Interpretation: Momentum represents “quantity of motion” — how hard it is to stop a moving object.

Newton’s Second Law (Reformulated)

Newton’s law can be expressed as the rate of change of momentum:

Fnet = dp/dt

This means that forces are not just accelerations, but agents that change momentum.

2. Impulse and the Impulse–Momentum Theorem

Impulse (J) is the product of average force and the time interval of application:

J = Favg · Δt

The Impulse–Momentum Theorem states:

J = Δp = m vf − m vi
  • Impulse changes momentum; a large force over a short time can equal a small force over a long time.
  • Force–Time Graph: The area under the curve equals impulse.
Example: Airbags increase collision time, reducing the force experienced, while keeping impulse (and Δp) constant.

3. Conservation of Momentum

In an isolated system with no external net force:

Σpinitial = Σpfinal
  • Applies to: collisions, explosions, recoil of guns, rocket propulsion.
  • Closed system: mass is constant, external forces negligible.

4. Types of Collisions

Elastic Collisions

  • Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.
  • Typical in ideal gas molecules or billiard balls (approx.).

Inelastic Collisions

  • Momentum conserved, kinetic energy not conserved.
  • Energy dissipates into heat, sound, deformation.

Perfectly Inelastic Collisions

  • Objects stick together after collision.
  • Maximum kinetic energy loss while still conserving momentum.
Example: Two railway cars coupling together demonstrate a perfectly inelastic collision.

5. Advanced Topics

Center of Mass Frame

Analyzing collisions in the center-of-mass frame simplifies momentum conservation calculations.

Rocket Propulsion

Momentum conservation explains how rockets accelerate by expelling mass backward (conservation law applied to exhaust gases).

Relativistic Momentum

At speeds near light, momentum is modified: p = γmv, with γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²).

Applications in Particle Physics

High-energy collisions in particle accelerators rely on momentum conservation to deduce unseen particle properties.

6. Summary and Reflection

  • Momentum quantifies motion and is conserved in isolated systems.
  • Impulse is the mechanism that changes momentum, relating force and time.
  • Collisions reveal conservation laws and energy transfer dynamics.
  • Applications span from airbags to rocket science to particle accelerators.

Studying momentum and impulse reveals that even simple Newtonian concepts scale to advanced domains like astrophysics and quantum fields.
