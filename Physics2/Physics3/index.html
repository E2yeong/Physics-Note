Physics 3 — Circular Motion & Gravitation

Uniform & non-uniform circular motion, banked curves, vertical loops, Newtonian gravity, orbits, energy

Posted on 2025.09.11

1) Uniform Circular Motion (UCM)

Definition & kinematics

  • Object moves in a circle of radius \(R\) at constant speed \(v\). Direction of velocity changes continuously \(\Rightarrow\) the motion is accelerated.
  • Angular quantities: \(\omega=\dfrac{d\theta}{dt}\) (rad/s), period \(T=\dfrac{2\pi}{\omega}\), frequency \(f=\dfrac{1}{T}=\dfrac{\omega}{2\pi}\).
  • Linear–angular links: \(\displaystyle v=\omega R,\quad a_c=\dfrac{v^2}{R}=\omega^2 R.\)
Centripetal acceleration: always points toward the center. It changes direction of \(\vec v\) but not its magnitude in UCM.

Centripetal force

By Newton’s 2nd law, the required inward net force is \[ F_c = m a_c = m\,\frac{v^2}{R}=m\,\omega^2 R. \] “Centripetal force” is not a new kind of force; it is the role played by tension, normal force, gravity, friction, etc., as needed to bend the trajectory.

If the inward force drops to zero, the object leaves the circle along the instantaneous tangent (Newton’s 1st law).

2) Non-Uniform Circular Motion

When speed changes, acceleration has two components:

  • Tangential: \(a_t=\dfrac{dv}{dt}\) (changes speed).
  • Radial/centripetal: \(a_c=\dfrac{v^2}{R}\) (changes direction).

Total acceleration: \(\displaystyle \vec a = \vec a_t + \vec a_c\) with magnitude \(\sqrt{a_t^2+a_c^2}\).

3) Classic Applications

3.1 Level (unbanked) curve — friction provides centripetal force

Max safe speed (no slipping) on a flat turn of radius \(R\):

\[ f_s^{\max} = \mu_s N = \mu_s m g \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{m v_{\max}^2}{R} \le \mu_s m g \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_{\max} = \sqrt{\mu_s g R}. \]

3.2 Banked curve (idealized, no friction)

For bank angle \(\theta\), normal force supplies the needed horizontal component:

\[ \tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{gR} \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = \sqrt{g R \tan\theta}. \]

With friction, the usable range of \(v\) widens; direction of friction (up or down the slope) depends on whether \(v\) is below or above the ideal \( \sqrt{gR\tan\theta}\).

3.3 Conical pendulum

Mass \(m\) moves in a horizontal circle of radius \(R\), string makes angle \(\theta\) with vertical, tension \(T\):

\[ T\cos\theta = mg,\qquad T\sin\theta = \frac{m v^2}{R} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{gR}. \]

3.4 Vertical circle (loop)

  • Top of loop (track contact): \(N_{\text{top}} + mg = \dfrac{m v_{\text{top}}^2}{R}\). For just maintaining contact: \(N_{\text{top}}=0 \Rightarrow v_{\text{top}} \ge \sqrt{gR}\).
  • Bottom: \(N_{\text{bot}} - mg = \dfrac{m v_{\text{bot}}^2}{R}\) \(\Rightarrow N_{\text{bot}} = mg + \dfrac{m v_{\text{bot}}^2}{R}\) (largest normal force).
Apparent weight equals the normal force. It can be smaller (even zero) at the top and larger at the bottom than the true weight \(mg\).

4) Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation

Law

\[ F_g = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}, \qquad G \approx 6.674\times 10^{-11}\ \text{N·m}^2\!/\text{kg}^2. \] Force is attractive and acts along the line joining the two masses (action–reaction pair has equal magnitude).

Gravitational field and local \(g\)

Gravitational field of mass \(M\) at distance \(r\): \(\displaystyle g(r) = \frac{GM}{r^2}\) (points toward \(M\)). Near Earth’s surface, \[ g \approx \frac{G M_{\oplus}}{R_{\oplus}^2}. \] \(g\) decreases with altitude; it is not strictly constant.

Potential energy (two-body)

\[ U(r) = -\,\frac{G M m}{r} \quad (\text{choose }U\to 0\text{ as }r\to\infty). \] More negative \(U\) means a more tightly bound system. Work by gravity from \(r_1\) to \(r_2\) equals \(\Delta K = -\Delta U\).

5) Orbits: Circular Speed, Period, Energy

Gravity as centripetal force

For a small mass \(m\) orbiting a large mass \(M\) (e.g., satellite–Earth), set gravity equal to required centripetal force:

\[ \frac{G M m}{r^2} = \frac{m v^2}{r} \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_{\text{orbit}} = \sqrt{\frac{G M}{r}}. \]

Orbital period: \[ T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{r^3}{G M}}, \] which is Kepler’s 3rd law for circular orbits.

Mechanical energy in a circular orbit

Total energy \(E=K+U\): \[ K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = \frac{G M m}{2r}, \qquad U = -\frac{G M m}{r}, \qquad E = -\frac{G M m}{2r}. \] Bound circular orbits have negative total energy; tighter orbits (smaller \(r\)) are more negative.

Escape speed

Minimum speed (ignoring atmosphere/rotation) to reach \(r\to\infty\) with zero final speed:

\[ v_{\text{esc}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 G M}{r}} = \sqrt{2}\,v_{\text{orbit}}.

Common orbits:
  • LEO (Low Earth Orbit): \(r \approx 1.05\,R_\oplus\) to \(2\,R_\oplus\); \(T \sim 90\) minutes.
  • GEO (Geostationary): \(T=1\) sidereal day; \(r \approx 42{,}164\) km from Earth’s center (\(\sim 35{,}786\) km altitude).

6) Worked Examples

Example 1 — Banked turn

What bank angle \(\theta\) allows a car to take a curve of radius \(R=50\,\text{m}\) at \(v=18\,\text{m/s}\) with no friction?

Use \(\tan\theta=\dfrac{v^2}{gR}\Rightarrow \theta=\arctan\!\left(\dfrac{18^2}{9.8\times 50}\right)\approx \arctan(0.662)\approx 33.5^\circ.\)

Example 2 — Minimum loop speed at the top

A bead of mass \(m\) slides frictionlessly on a loop of radius \(R=1.2\,\text{m}\). What minimum speed at the top prevents losing contact?

Set \(N_{\text{top}}=0\Rightarrow v_{\text{top}}=\sqrt{gR}=\sqrt{9.8\times 1.2}\approx 3.43\,\text{m/s}.\)

Example 3 — Satellite circular speed

Find \(v\) for a satellite in a circular orbit \(300\,\text{km}\) above Earth’s surface.

Use \(r=R_\oplus+300\,\text{km}\), \(v=\sqrt{\dfrac{GM_\oplus}{r}}\). With \(R_\oplus\approx 6.371\times 10^6\,\text{m}\), \(GM_\oplus\approx 3.986\times10^{14}\,\text{m}^3/\text{s}^2\), \(r\approx 6.671\times 10^6\,\text{m}\Rightarrow v\approx 7.73\,\text{km/s}.\)

7) Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. Diagram: Draw the path, choose a coordinate frame (radial ↔ tangential), and mark knowns/unknowns.
  2. Free-body diagram: Identify forces. Decide which component supplies centripetal role (toward center).
  3. Kinematics: For UCM, use \(v=\omega R\), \(a_c=v^2/R\). For non-UCM, include \(a_t\).
  4. Dynamics: Apply \(\sum F_r = m v^2/R\) (radial) and \(\sum F_t = m a_t\) (tangential).
  5. Energy (when helpful): Use \(K,\,U=-GMm/r\), conservation if no non-conservative work.
  6. Check units & limits: Ensure answers behave sensibly as \(R\to\infty\), \(v\to 0\), etc.

8) Quick Reference (Formulas)

Circular kinematics

  • \(v=\omega R\)
  • \(a_c=\dfrac{v^2}{R}=\omega^2 R\)
  • \(T=\dfrac{2\pi}{\omega},\; f=\dfrac{1}{T}\)

Forces & curves

  • \(F_c=m \dfrac{v^2}{R}\)
  • Flat turn: \(v_{\max}=\sqrt{\mu_s g R}\)
  • Banked (no friction): \(v=\sqrt{gR\tan\theta}\)
  • Vertical loop (top contact): \(v_{\text{top}}\ge\sqrt{gR}\)

Gravitation & orbits

  • \(F_g=G\dfrac{Mm}{r^2}\)
  • \(g(r)=\dfrac{GM}{r^2}\)
  • \(U(r)=-\dfrac{GMm}{r}\)
  • \(v_{\text{orbit}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{GM}{r}}\)
  • \(T=2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{r^3}{GM}}\)
  • \(v_{\text{esc}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{2GM}{r}}\)

9) Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing “centripetal force” with a new force type — it’s the net inward force, often composed of familiar forces.
  • For vertical circles, forgetting that normal force can drop to zero at the top; use contact condition \(N\ge 0\).
  • Using \(g=9.8\,\text{m/s}^2\) at high altitude. Use \(g(r)=GM/r^2\) for orbital problems.
  • Mixing up speed \(v\) with angular speed \(\omega\): check \(v=\omega R\).
  • For friction problems, ensuring you use static friction limit for non-slip circular motion on a flat road.
